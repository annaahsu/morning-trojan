</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><i>This is a guest column. All three authors are tenured professors at USC.</i></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">Five months ago, USC announced something that a series of sweeping budget cuts had already signaled: It was facing a structural deficit of $158 million, and more pain would be coming. As Provost Andrew Guzman</span><span style="font-weight:700;"><b> </b></span><a class="link" href="https://www.uscannenbergmedia.com/2024/11/20/carol-folt-says-we-dont-know-how-the-trump-administration-will-affect-usc/?utm_source=morningtrojan.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=usc-is-in-a-financial-crisis-why-are-staff-and-faculty-shouldering-the-burden" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" style="-webkit-text-decoration:underline #ac2124;color:#ac2124;text-decoration:underline #ac2124;word-break:break-word;;">then told</a><span style=""> the faculty senate, “Difficult decisions are going to have to be made.”</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">Matters have since gotten worse. USC now faces cuts to federal research funding and potential decreases in international student enrollment due to the Trump administration’s policies. Leadership </span><a class="link" href="https://morningtrojan.com/p/live-usc-layoff-and-budget-cut-tracker" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" style="-webkit-text-decoration:underline #ac2124;color:#ac2124;text-decoration:underline #ac2124;word-break:break-word;;">has responded</a><span style=""> to this perfect storm of budget pressure by freezing faculty and staff wages, halting hiring, and suspending many investments in student learning, including the purchase of certain library books and all new database and journal subscriptions. </span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">It will undoubtedly take shared sacrifice to weather the next four years of this administration. But at USC, it seems that sacrifice is coming from the bottom up. </span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"> This year, with a few limited exceptions, no USC employees will receive a merit raise<span style="">. The so-called austerity measure was introduced alongside </span><a class="link" href="https://morningtrojan.com/p/usc-planned-benefit-cuts" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" style="-webkit-text-decoration:underline #ac2124;color:#ac2124;text-decoration:underline #ac2124;word-break:break-word;;">notable cuts</a><span style=""> to employee benefits </span>and increases in out-of-pocket healthcare costs<span style="">. Meanwhile, USC’s administrators, many of whom earn seven-figure salaries, have not committed to eschewing their own contractually-defined pay raises.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">IRS data shows that in the 2022-2023 academic year (the most recent year available), 18 USC executives made over $1 million. This includes $3 million paid to former President Max Nikias, along with the over $3.5 million base salary paid to our current president — not including housing and other perks.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">We propose that the university’s top earners commit to freezing their wages and taking pay cuts. This will ensure that USC does not balance its budget on the backs of our most vulnerable staff and faculty.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">There is precedent for this. In 2020, at the onset of the pandemic, the president took a </span><span style=""><a class="link" href="https://www.provost.usc.edu/an-important-message-from-the-provost-and-chief-financial-officer/?utm_source=morningtrojan.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=usc-is-in-a-financial-crisis-why-are-staff-and-faculty-shouldering-the-burden" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" style="-webkit-text-decoration:underline #ac2124;color:#ac2124;text-decoration:underline #ac2124;word-break:break-word;;">20% reduction in salary</a></span><span style="">, and the provost, senior vice presidents, and deans reduced their salaries by 10%, while reinstating pay increases for employees making less than $100,000 per year.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">But in 2025, when presented with this idea at faculty senate meetings and via email, the administration has variously dodged the question, admitted that there has been no discussion about the option, or stated that they’ve already detailed their plans to address “financial resiliency.”</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">“As things continue to evolve at the federal and local level, we will regularly assess how we are progressing and whether further actions or adjustments are needed,” a university spokesperson wrote when asked for comment on this column.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">USC’s plan to equally cut salaries and benefits across the university — excluding unionized and contractually obligated employees — is regressive by its very nature, and will fall much harder on lower-paid workers.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">University administrators are responsible for shouldering this burden, not just because they are paid more than anybody else, but because they are charged with stewardship of the school and its financial health.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">We are told that administrators’ salaries are justified because of their responsibilities. But by that logic, if the institution is on the financial rocks — which we know was the case even before the election — that is on some level their doing: their failure to plan or anticipate.</span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
</style><div style="padding-bottom:12px;padding-left:10px;padding-right:10px;padding-top:12px;"><p style="color:#000000;color:var(--wt-text-on-background-color) !important;font-family:'Helvetica',Arial,sans-serif;font-size:19px;line-height:1.5;text-align:left;"><span style="">Cuts for the rest of us in this context are especially unacceptable. If USC’s executive ranks are truly committed to our community, they will take the same pay cuts they took during the COVID-19 crisis. </span></p></div><style>
  p span[style*="font-size"] { line-height: 1.6; }
